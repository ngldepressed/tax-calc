📊 How Progressive Tax Brackets Work

The U.S. uses a progressive tax system — you don't pay the top rate on all your income. Each dollar is taxed only at the rate of the bracket it falls into.

Example: Single filer with $80,000 taxable income (2025)
First $11,92510% = $1,193
$11,926 – $48,47512% = $4,386
$48,476 – $80,00022% = $6,935
Total Tax$12,514 (eff. rate ~15.6%)

Your marginal rate is 22% but your effective rate is only 15.6%. These are very different numbers.

🔢 Marginal vs. Effective Rate

Marginal rate = the rate on your last dollar of income. People often think they "jump into a bracket" and pay that rate on everything — this is a myth.

Effective rate = total tax ÷ total income. This is your actual average tax burden.

💡 A raise that puts you in a higher bracket does not cause you to pay more on prior income. Only the dollars above the threshold are taxed at the higher rate.

🔄 Gross Income → AGI → Taxable Income

Your taxes are calculated on taxable income, not gross income. Here's the flow:

1
Gross Income — wages, tips, overtime, capital gains, SE income, SS benefits
2
Above-the-Line Deductions — 401(k), IRA, HSA, SE tax ½, student loan interest → gives you AGI
3
Standard or Itemized Deduction — subtracted from AGI → gives you Taxable Income
4
Tax calculated using brackets on taxable income
5
Credits applied → reduces your final tax bill dollar-for-dollar

🏦 FICA / Payroll Taxes

On top of income tax, most workers pay FICA (Federal Insurance Contributions Act) taxes:

Social Security: 6.2% on wages up to $176,100 (2025). Your employer matches this.
Medicare: 1.45% on all wages. No wage cap. Employer matches.
Additional Medicare: 0.9% on wages/SE income above $200k (single) / $250k (MFJ) — employee only, no match.
Self-employed: Pay both halves = 15.3% SE tax (but deduct 50% above-the-line).

📉 Standard Deduction vs. Itemizing

You choose one or the other each year — whatever gives you a bigger deduction wins. Since the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act dramatically raised the standard deduction, about 90% of taxpayers now take the standard deduction.

Standard Deduction (2025)

Single: $15,750
Married Filing Jointly: $31,500
Head of Household: $23,625
Additional $1,600 if age 65+ or blind (single); $1,350 per qualifying person (MFJ)

Common Itemized Deductions (Schedule A)

Mortgage interest — on up to $750,000 of loan
State & local taxes (SALT) — capped at $10,000/year
Charitable contributions — cash & non-cash donations
Medical expenses — exceeding 7.5% of AGI
Casualty losses — federally declared disasters only

📋 Above-the-Line Deductions (Schedule 1)

These reduce your AGI before you even get to the standard deduction — making them especially powerful because a lower AGI also helps with credit phase-outs and state taxes.

✅ 401(k)/403(b)/457: Up to $23,500 (2025); $31,000 if 50+
✅ Traditional IRA: Up to $7,000 ($8,000 if 50+). Deductibility phases out if covered by workplace plan and income is above ~$79k (single) / ~$126k (MFJ)
✅ HSA: $4,300 single / $8,550 family. Must have HDHP.
✅ SEP-IRA / Solo 401(k): Up to 25% of net SE income, max $70,000 (2025)
✅ Student loan interest: Up to $2,500, phases out above $80k
✅ Alimony: Pre-2019 divorce agreements only
✅ ½ SE Tax: Automatic deduction for self-employed

🏠 SALT Deduction Cap

The State and Local Tax (SALT) deduction allows itemizers to deduct state income tax, property taxes, and local taxes — but it's capped at $10,000 per household (regardless of filing status).

This cap, introduced in the TCJA 2017, hit high-tax states like California, New York, and New Jersey particularly hard.

💡 The SALT cap is one reason high earners in CA/NY often still benefit from itemizing — their mortgage interest + property taxes can still exceed the standard deduction even with SALT capped at $10k.

The OBBB Act did not permanently raise the SALT cap; its status for 2025+ remains $10,000.

🎓 Education Deductions & Credits

American Opportunity Credit (AOTC): Up to $2,500/year for first 4 years of college. 40% refundable. Phases out $80k–$90k (single).
Lifetime Learning Credit (LLC): 20% of up to $10,000 tuition. Not refundable. No year limit. Phases out $80k–$90k.
Student Loan Interest: Up to $2,500 above-the-line. Phases out $85k–$100k (single, 2025).
529 Plans: State-sponsored. Contributions not federally deductible but growth is tax-free. Expanded to K-12 and apprenticeships.

🏢 Business Deductions (Schedule C)

Self-employed individuals can deduct ordinary and necessary business expenses from gross income before calculating SE tax. Common deductions:

Home office: Simplified method ($5/sq ft, max 300 sq ft) or actual expense method
Vehicle: Standard mileage (67¢/mile in 2024) or actual expenses
Health insurance premiums: 100% deductible above-the-line for self-employed
Software & subscriptions: Business tools fully deductible
Meals: 50% deductible for business meals with clients
Section 179 / Bonus Depreciation: Immediate deduction of equipment/property costs

💳 Tax Credits vs. Tax Deductions — What's the Difference?

Deduction

Reduces your taxable income. The benefit equals the deduction × your tax rate. A $1,000 deduction at a 22% rate = $220 in actual tax savings.

Credit

Reduces your tax bill dollar-for-dollar. A $1,000 credit = exactly $1,000 less in taxes. Credits are almost always more valuable than equal deductions.

💡 Refundable credits can reduce your tax below $0 — meaning you get money back even if you owe no tax. Non-refundable credits can only reduce your tax to $0.

👶 Child Tax Credit (CTC)

$2,200 per qualifying child under 17 (2025+, OBBB §70101 — increased from $2,000).

Non-refundable portion: Reduces tax to $0
Refundable (ACTC): Up to $1,700/child (2025). 15% of earned income above $2,500.
Phase-out: Begins at $200k (single) / $400k (MFJ). Reduces $50 per $1,000 over threshold.
Eligibility: Child must be under 17, have valid SSN, live with you 6+ months.

⚡ Energy Credits (IRA 2022)

The Inflation Reduction Act created several valuable energy credits:

Residential Clean Energy Credit: 30% of solar panels, wind, geothermal, battery storage. No cap. Runs through 2032.
Energy Efficient Home Improvement: 30% of heat pumps, insulation, windows, doors. Capped at $1,200/year ($2,000 for heat pumps).
Clean Vehicle Credit: Up to $7,500 for new EVs. Income limits apply ($150k single). Used EV credit: up to $4,000.

💰 Saver's Credit

A credit for low-to-moderate income individuals who contribute to 401(k), IRA, or ABLE accounts. Worth 10%–50% of contributions up to $2,000 ($4,000 MFJ).

Phase-out for 2025: $38,250 (single) / $76,500 (MFJ). Fully non-refundable — can only offset tax owed.

🏠 First-Time Homebuyer Credit

The original credit expired, but current provisions include:

Mortgage Interest Deduction: Itemizers deduct interest on up to $750k mortgage
Points paid at closing: Deductible in the year paid for main home purchase
Property tax: Deductible up to $10,000 SALT cap
IRA penalty exception: First-time buyers can withdraw up to $10,000 from IRA penalty-free

⚡ The One Big Beautiful Bill (OBBB Act) — What Changed

The OBBB Act introduced major above-the-line deductions effective for tax year 2025 and beyond. These are in addition to — not replacing — existing deductions.

What the OBBB Changed

✅ Overtime deduction (new — first ever)
✅ Tips deduction (new — expanded)
✅ Senior deduction ($6,000 per qualifying senior)
✅ Increased CTC to $2,200/child
✅ Increased ACTC to $1,700 (2025 & 2026)

What OBBB Did NOT Change

❌ SALT cap — still $10,000
❌ Tax rates or brackets (those adjust via IRS inflation)
❌ SE tax rates or structure
❌ AMT exemptions (separate IRS adjustment)

🕐 Overtime Deduction — OBBB §80001

For the first time in U.S. tax history, overtime pay can be deducted above-the-line. Applies to FLSA-covered overtime (hours beyond 40/week at 1.5x rate).

Cap: $12,500 (single) / $25,000 (MFJ)
Phase-out: Reduces $1 per $2 of gross income above $150,000 (single) / $300,000 (MFJ)
Who qualifies: FLSA non-exempt hourly and salaried employees. Tip: Independent contractors and salaried-exempt workers typically don't get FLSA OT.
How to claim: Enter on Schedule 1, Line 24 (new line added for 2025)
Example: $60k salary, $8,000 OT at 22% rate
OT Deduction$8,000 (full, under $12,500 cap)
Tax saved at 22%$1,760

💵 Tips Deduction — OBBB §80001

Workers in customarily tipped occupations can now deduct all qualified tip income above-the-line. Same phase-out structure as the overtime deduction.

Qualifying occupations: Servers, bartenders, hairdressers, taxi/rideshare drivers, hotel staff, casino dealers, delivery workers — any occupation where tips are customary and expected.
Does NOT qualify: Tips received by lawyers, doctors, accountants, consultants, or other professional service workers.
Must be reported: Tips must still be properly reported to employers (Form 4070) and on your W-2.
No dollar cap on qualified tips (unlike OT which is capped). Only limited by the phase-out.

👴 Senior Deduction — OBBB (Temporary, expires 2028)

A new above-the-line deduction of $6,000 per qualifying person age 65 or older as of December 31 of the tax year.

MFJ: Both spouses can each claim $6,000 = up to $12,000 total
Phase-out: Reduces 6¢ per dollar of gross income above $75,000 (single) / $150,000 (MFJ). Fully phased out ~$175k single / $250k MFJ.
Sunset: Expires after tax year 2028 unless extended
Social Security note: This deduction can reduce your provisional income, potentially making less of your SS benefits taxable

📅 OBBB — Expiration Dates & Sunset Provisions

⏳ Senior Deduction: Expires after 2028. Congress must act to extend.
✅ OT & Tips Deduction: No sunset stated — currently permanent unless repealed.
✅ $2,200 CTC: No sunset — permanently updated from $2,000.
⏳ TCJA provisions: Many TCJA elements (higher standard deduction, 37% top rate, 20% QBI) were scheduled to expire after 2025 but the OBBB Act effectively extended core provisions.
💡 Tax legislation is rarely permanent. Always check for updates from the IRS and Congress each year.

🏦 HSA — The Triple Tax Advantage

Health Savings Accounts are the only account in the tax code with three tax benefits simultaneously, making them extraordinarily powerful:

✅ Pre-tax contributions — reduces AGI (or excludes from paycheck income)
✅ Tax-free growth — invest inside the HSA just like a brokerage account
✅ Tax-free withdrawals for qualified medical expenses (now or in retirement)
⚡ After age 65: withdraw for any reason (taxed as ordinary income, like a traditional IRA)

Requires a High Deductible Health Plan (HDHP). Minimum deductible 2025: $1,650 single / $3,300 family.

📊 Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs)

The IRS requires you to start withdrawing from pre-tax retirement accounts beginning at age 73 (SECURE 2.0 Act). The amount is calculated by dividing your account balance by an IRS life expectancy factor.

Applies to: Traditional IRA, 401(k), 403(b), SEP-IRA, SIMPLE IRA
Does NOT apply to: Roth IRA (during your lifetime)
Penalty for missing RMD: 25% excise tax on the shortfall (reduced to 10% if corrected promptly)
QCD strategy: If 70½+, donate up to $105,000/year directly from IRA to charity — counts toward RMD and excluded from income.

🔄 Backdoor Roth IRA

For high earners above the Roth contribution limit, a legal two-step strategy:

1
Contribute to a non-deductible traditional IRA (no income limit)
2
Immediately convert to Roth IRA (no income limit on conversions)
3
Pay tax only on any gains between contribution and conversion (usually $0 if done immediately)

The "pro-rata rule" applies if you have other pre-tax IRA balances — consult a tax professional if so.

🧾 Self-Employment Tax — The Full Picture

When you're self-employed, you pay both sides of FICA — the employee's share and the employer's share — totaling 15.3%. But there are deductions to offset this.

1
Net SE income × 92.35% = SE earnings (accounts for the employer-half deduction)
2
SE Tax = 12.4% SS (on SE earnings up to $176,100) + 2.9% Medicare (all SE earnings) = 15.3%
3
Deduct 50% of SE tax above-the-line (Schedule 1 Line 15) — reduces AGI
4
QBI Deduction (§199A): Deduct 20% of qualified business income from taxable income
Example: $80,000 net SE income
SE Earnings (×92.35%)$73,880
SE Tax (15.3%)$11,304

📋 Section 199A — QBI Deduction

The 20% Qualified Business Income deduction is one of the most valuable tax breaks for the self-employed. It reduces taxable income by 20% of net business income.

Income threshold (2025): Below $197,300 (single) / $394,600 (MFJ) — QBI is simple: take 20%.
Above threshold: Limited by 50% of W-2 wages paid by the business, or 25% of W-2 wages + 2.5% of qualified property.
SSTB limitation: Specified Service Trades (law, finance, consulting, health, performing arts) cannot claim QBI above the income threshold — fully phased out $50k above threshold.

📅 Quarterly Estimated Taxes

The IRS operates a pay-as-you-go system. If you're self-employed or have significant non-wage income, you must make quarterly payments to avoid underpayment penalties.

Due dates: Apr 15, Jun 16, Sep 15, Jan 15 (for 2025 tax year)
Safe harbor — Option A: Pay 90% of current year's expected tax
Safe harbor — Option B: Pay 100% of prior year's total tax (110% if prior year AGI >$150k)
Form to use: IRS Form 1040-ES
Penalty rate 2025: Federal funds rate + 3% (currently ~8%/year on underpayments)

🏗️ The "Stacking" Rule for Capital Gains

Capital gains are stacked on top of ordinary income when determining which CG rate applies. This is critical for planning.

Example: $40k salary + $30k long-term capital gains (single, 2025)
Ordinary income$40,000
0% CG threshold$48,350
Space in 0% bracket$8,350
Taxed at 0%$8,350 → $0 tax
Remaining at 15%$21,650 → $3,248 tax

🏠 Real Estate Capital Gains Exclusion

When you sell your primary residence, you can exclude up to $250,000 ($500,000 MFJ) of capital gains from tax.

Requirements: Owned and lived in as primary home for 2 of last 5 years
Frequency: Can only use once every 2 years
Depreciation recapture: If you ever claimed depreciation (rental use), that portion is recaptured at 25%
Partial exclusion: Available if move was due to health, employment, or unforeseen circumstances

🔄 Tax-Loss Harvesting

Selling investments at a loss to offset capital gains — a powerful year-end strategy.

How it works: Capital losses offset capital gains dollar-for-dollar. Net losses up to $3,000/year can offset ordinary income.
Carryforward: Excess losses carry forward indefinitely to future years.
Wash-sale rule: Cannot buy the same or "substantially identical" security within 30 days before/after the sale (losses disallowed).
Crypto: Currently NOT subject to wash-sale rules — a unique planning opportunity.

📊 Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT)

A 3.8% surtax on net investment income for high earners, introduced by the ACA. Applies to the lesser of: net investment income OR the amount by which MAGI exceeds the threshold.

Threshold: $200,000 (single) / $250,000 (MFJ)
What counts: Interest, dividends, capital gains, rental income, passive business income
What doesn't: Wages, SE income, distributions from retirement accounts
💡 At $300k income (single): effective top capital gains rate = 20% (LTCG) + 3.8% (NIIT) = 23.8%

⚠️ Alternative Minimum Tax — How It Works

The AMT is a parallel tax system designed to ensure high earners pay a minimum amount of tax, regardless of deductions. You calculate tax twice and pay whichever is higher.

1
Calculate your regular income tax using brackets + all deductions
2
Calculate AMTI (Alternative Minimum Taxable Income) — add back certain "preference items" like the standard deduction
3
Subtract the AMT exemption ($88,100 single / $137,000 MFJ for 2025)
4
Apply AMT rates: 26% on AMTI up to $232,600; 28% above that
5
Pay the higher of regular tax or AMT (the excess over regular tax is "AMT owed")

🎯 Who Gets Hit by AMT?

After TCJA 2017 significantly raised exemptions, AMT primarily affects:

High earners: Income roughly $400k–$750k (single) — above exemption phase-out but below where regular brackets are clearly higher
ISO exercisers: Exercising Incentive Stock Options is a major AMT preference item
Large SALT deductions: State income taxes are not deductible under AMT
Accelerated depreciation: Certain business deductions differ under AMT
💡 The TCJA 2017 changes made very few ordinary wage earners subject to AMT. It mainly targets specific tax planning strategies.

🔄 AMT Credit (MTC)

When you pay AMT due to timing differences (like ISO exercises), you earn a Minimum Tax Credit you can use in future years when your regular tax exceeds AMT.

Think of it as prepaid tax — you get credit for AMT paid on deferral items once those items eventually show up in regular income.

AMT paid on exclusion items (like standard deduction add-back) does NOT generate a credit.

Above-the-Line Deduction

Deductions subtracted from gross income to calculate AGI, regardless of whether you itemize. More powerful than itemized deductions because they reduce AGI, which affects credit phase-outs and state taxes. Examples: 401(k), IRA, HSA, SE tax deduction, student loan interest, OBBB overtime/tips/senior deductions.

ACTC — Additional Child Tax Credit

The refundable portion of the Child Tax Credit. If the non-refundable CTC exceeds your tax liability, the remaining credit (up to $1,700/child in 2025) can be refunded as cash. Calculated as 15% of earned income above $2,500.

AGI — Adjusted Gross Income

Gross income minus above-the-line deductions. AGI appears on Line 11 of Form 1040 and is the key number used to determine eligibility for credits, deductions, and phase-outs. A lower AGI generally means lower taxes and more benefits.

AMT — Alternative Minimum Tax

A parallel federal tax system that disallows certain deductions and applies flat rates (26%/28%) to ensure high earners pay a minimum tax. You calculate both regular tax and AMT, then pay whichever is higher. The difference between AMT and regular tax is the "additional AMT owed."

AMTI — Alternative Minimum Taxable Income

The income base for AMT calculations. Starts with regular taxable income and adds back "preference items" like the standard deduction, ISO stock options, and certain depreciation. After subtracting the AMT exemption, the flat AMT rates are applied.

At-Risk Rules & Passive Activity

IRS rules limiting deductible losses from investments and businesses. Passive activity losses (from businesses you don't materially participate in) can only offset passive income, not wages. Rental real estate has special rules — losses up to $25,000/year allowed if AGI is below $100,000.

Bonus Depreciation / Section 179

Provisions allowing businesses to immediately deduct the full cost of qualifying equipment, machinery, or property in the year of purchase rather than depreciating over multiple years. Section 179 has a $1.22M limit (2024); bonus depreciation allows 60% in 2024, phasing down to 0% by 2027 under current law.

Tax Brackets (Progressive)

The U.S. federal income tax system divides income into ranges, each taxed at a higher rate than the last. Only the income within each bracket range is taxed at that rate — not all income. 2025 has 7 brackets: 10%, 12%, 22%, 24%, 32%, 35%, 37%. Brackets are adjusted annually for inflation.

Capital Gains (Short vs. Long-Term)

Profit from selling an asset. Short-term (held ≤1 year) is taxed as ordinary income at bracket rates. Long-term (held >1 year) is taxed at preferential rates: 0%, 15%, or 20%. Capital gains are "stacked on top of" ordinary income to determine which rate applies.

Carryforward / Carryover

When a loss or credit exceeds what can be used in the current year, the unused amount is "carried forward" to future tax years. Capital loss carryforwards offset future gains; NOL carryforwards offset future income; AMT credit carryforwards offset future regular tax.

CTC — Child Tax Credit

A credit of $2,200 per qualifying child under 17 (updated by OBBB Act for 2025+). Reduces federal income tax dollar-for-dollar. The non-refundable portion reduces tax to $0; the refundable portion (ACTC) can result in a cash refund. Phases out above $200,000 (single) / $400,000 (MFJ).

Constructive Receipt

Income is taxable when you have an unrestricted right to receive it — even if you don't actually receive it yet. For example, a check mailed on Dec 31 is income in that year even if you don't cash it until January. This principle prevents taxpayers from deferring income by simply not collecting it.

Cost Basis / Step-Up in Basis

Cost basis is what you paid for an asset (plus improvements), subtracted from the sale price to calculate capital gains. When you inherit an asset, you receive a "stepped-up basis" equal to the fair market value at the date of death — eliminating capital gains tax on appreciation during the deceased's lifetime.

Credits: Refundable vs. Non-Refundable

Non-refundable credits reduce tax to $0 but no further. Refundable credits can reduce tax below $0, resulting in a cash refund. Partially refundable credits (like CTC) have both components. Credits are always more valuable than equivalent deductions because they reduce tax directly, not just income.

Deductions: Standard vs. Itemized

You choose one each year. Standard deduction is a flat amount ($15,750 single 2025). Itemized deductions (Schedule A) list specific expenses: mortgage interest, state/local taxes (SALT, capped at $10k), charitable donations, and qualifying medical expenses. Take whichever is larger.

Depreciation Recapture (§1250)

When you sell property you previously depreciated (e.g., a rental home), the IRS "recaptures" those depreciation deductions as ordinary income (for personal property) or at a special 25% rate (for real property). This prevents double-dipping on deductions that already reduced taxable income.

Domicile / Tax Residency

Your legal home state for tax purposes — where you intend to return and make your permanent home. Most states tax based on domicile. High-tax states like CA and NY aggressively audit domicile claims and may assert you were a resident if you spent 183+ days there or maintained significant ties.

EIC / EITC — Earned Income Credit

A fully refundable federal credit for low-to-moderate income workers. Amount depends on earned income, filing status, and number of children. Maximum $8,046 (3+ children, 2025). Phases out at higher incomes. Investment income above $11,000 disqualifies you. One of the largest government benefit programs.

Effective Tax Rate

Total tax paid ÷ total gross income. Your real average tax burden, not the marginal rate. Always lower than your marginal bracket rate because lower income is taxed at lower rates first. Example: $80k income, $12,514 tax = 15.6% effective rate even though marginal rate is 22%.

Estimated Quarterly Tax

Payments made four times per year (April 15, June 15, Sep 15, Jan 15) by self-employed individuals and those with significant non-wage income. Required when you expect to owe at least $1,000. Use IRS Form 1040-ES. Avoid underpayment penalties by meeting the "safe harbor" thresholds.

FICA — Federal Insurance Contributions Act

Payroll taxes for Social Security (6.2% on wages up to $176,100) and Medicare (1.45% on all wages). Both employer and employee pay each half. Self-employed pay the full 15.3% as SE tax (but deduct 50% above-the-line). Additional 0.9% Medicare applies on wages/SE income above $200k/$250k.

FLSA — Fair Labor Standards Act

Federal law requiring overtime pay (1.5× regular rate) for non-exempt employees who work over 40 hours/week. FLSA overtime is specifically required for the OBBB overtime deduction. Salaried-exempt employees and independent contractors are not covered by FLSA OT rules and generally cannot claim the OBBB OT deduction.

Key IRS Forms

Form 1040: Main individual tax return. W-2: Employer reports your wages and withholding. W-4: You tell your employer how much to withhold. 1099: Reports non-wage income (freelance, dividends, interest). Schedule A: Itemized deductions. Schedule C: Self-employment income/expenses. 1040-ES: Quarterly estimated payments.

Gross Income

All income from all sources before any deductions: wages, salaries, tips, overtime, self-employment income, capital gains, dividends, interest, rental income, alimony (pre-2019), and the taxable portion of Social Security benefits. Starting point for the tax calculation.

Head of Household (HOH)

A filing status for unmarried individuals who paid more than 50% of the cost of maintaining a home for a qualifying person for more than half the year. Gets a larger standard deduction ($23,625 in 2025) and lower brackets than single filers. Cannot be used if married.

HSA — Health Savings Account

A tax-advantaged account available to those enrolled in a High-Deductible Health Plan (HDHP). Contributions are pre-tax (reduce AGI), growth is tax-free, and withdrawals for qualified medical expenses are tax-free. After 65, can withdraw for any purpose (taxed like IRA). 2025 limits: $4,300 individual / $8,550 family.

Inflation Adjustment (Indexing)

Each year, the IRS adjusts tax brackets, standard deductions, credit amounts, and various thresholds for inflation using the Chained Consumer Price Index (C-CPI-U). This prevents "bracket creep" — where wage increases push workers into higher brackets even when real purchasing power doesn't increase.

IRA — Individual Retirement Account

Traditional IRA: Contributions may be deductible; withdrawals taxed as ordinary income; RMDs required at 73. Roth IRA: No deduction; growth and qualified withdrawals tax-free; no RMDs. 2025 contribution limit: $7,000 ($8,000 if 50+). Roth contribution phases out at $150k–$165k (single).

Itemized Deductions (Schedule A)

A list of specific deductible expenses you claim instead of the standard deduction. Requires more record-keeping. Main categories: mortgage interest (loans up to $750k), state/local taxes (SALT, capped at $10k), charitable contributions, and medical expenses exceeding 7.5% of AGI.

Kiddie Tax

Unearned income (dividends, interest, capital gains) over a threshold for children under 19 (or full-time students under 24) is taxed at the parent's marginal rate, not the child's lower rate. Designed to prevent income shifting to children in lower brackets. 2025 threshold: $2,500.

Marginal Tax Rate

The tax rate on your next (last) dollar of ordinary income — the highest bracket you fall into. Determines the value of deductions (a deduction saves you your marginal rate × deduction amount). Does NOT mean all income is taxed at this rate — only the income in that bracket range.

Medicare Surtax (NIIT) — 3.8%

The Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT) applies 3.8% to investment income for high earners: interest, dividends, capital gains, and passive rental income. Applies to the lesser of net investment income or the excess of MAGI over $200,000 (single) / $250,000 (MFJ). Introduced by the ACA.

MAGI — Modified Adjusted Gross Income

AGI with certain deductions added back. Different deductions are added back for different purposes. Used to determine: IRA deductibility, Roth IRA eligibility, ACA premium credits, NIIT threshold, student loan interest deduction, and more. The specific MAGI formula varies by benefit type.

Net Investment Income (NII)

Income from investments subject to the 3.8% NIIT: interest, ordinary dividends, qualified dividends, capital gains, rental income (passive), and annuity income. Does NOT include wages, SE income, active business income, retirement account distributions, or Social Security benefits.

OBBB Act — One Big Beautiful Bill

2025 legislation creating three new above-the-line deductions: (1) Overtime deduction — up to $12,500/$25,000 MFJ of FLSA OT pay; (2) Tips deduction — qualified tip income from customarily-tipped occupations; (3) Senior deduction — $6,000/person age 65+ (expires 2028). Also increased CTC to $2,200.

ODC — Other Dependent Credit

A $500 non-refundable credit per qualifying dependent who doesn't qualify for the full CTC. Examples: college students ages 17–23 you support, elderly parents living with you, or other relatives. No earned income requirement. Same phase-out thresholds as CTC.

Phase-Out

A gradual reduction in a deduction or credit as income increases beyond a threshold. Rather than an abrupt cutoff, phase-outs slowly reduce the benefit until it disappears. Example: CTC phases out $50 for every $1,000 of income above $200k, disappearing completely around $244k for a family with 2 children.

Provisional Income (SS Formula)

The number used to determine how much of Social Security benefits are taxable: AGI + tax-exempt interest + 50% of SS benefits. Below $25k (single): 0% of SS taxable. $25k–$34k: up to 50% taxable. Above $34k: up to 85% taxable. Never more than 85% of SS is subject to income tax.

QBI — Qualified Business Income Deduction (§199A)

Self-employed and pass-through business owners can deduct 20% of qualified business income from taxable income. Below income thresholds ($197,300 single 2025), the deduction is simple. Above thresholds, it's limited by W-2 wages paid. SSTB (law, finance, health, consulting) filers cannot claim QBI above thresholds.

RMD — Required Minimum Distribution

The IRS requires you to withdraw a minimum amount from pre-tax retirement accounts (Traditional IRA, 401k, 403b) starting at age 73 (SECURE 2.0). Amount = account balance ÷ IRS life expectancy factor. Roth IRAs have no RMDs. Penalty for missing an RMD: 25% excise tax (10% if corrected promptly).

Refund vs. Amount Owed

Your refund or balance due is determined by: Total Tax − Credits − Withholding. If withholding + credits > total tax, you get a refund. If total tax > withholding + credits, you owe the difference. A large refund means you over-withheld — you gave the IRS an interest-free loan all year.

Roth IRA / Roth Conversion

A Roth IRA uses after-tax money; growth and qualified withdrawals are 100% tax-free. No RMDs. Contribution income limits: phases out $150k–$165k (single 2025). Backdoor Roth: contribute to non-deductible IRA, then convert to Roth — legal workaround for high earners. Roth conversions are taxable in the year of conversion.

SALT — State and Local Tax Deduction

Itemizers can deduct state income taxes, property taxes, and local taxes paid during the year — but the total deduction is capped at $10,000 per household (regardless of filing status) since TCJA 2017. Hits hardest in high-tax, high-property-value states like CA, NY, and NJ.

Schedule C (Self-Employment)

The IRS form used by sole proprietors and single-member LLCs to report business income and deductible expenses. Net Schedule C profit flows directly to Form 1040 and is subject to both income tax and SE tax. Common deductions: home office, vehicle, equipment, software, health insurance, meals (50%).

SE Tax — Self-Employment Tax

15.3% tax (12.4% Social Security + 2.9% Medicare) paid by self-employed individuals on 92.35% of net SE income. Covers both the employee and employer halves of FICA. You deduct 50% of SE tax above-the-line. Additional 0.9% Medicare surtax applies if SE income exceeds $200k (single).

SEP-IRA / Solo 401(k)

Retirement plans for self-employed individuals. SEP-IRA: contribute up to 25% of net SE income, max $70,000 (2025). Solo 401(k): employee contribution up to $23,500 + employer contribution up to 25% of compensation, combined max $70,000. Both are pre-tax, reducing AGI significantly.

Social Security — Federal Taxability

Between 0% and 85% of Social Security benefits are taxable depending on "provisional income." Never 100% taxable. Lower-income retirees may owe tax on $0 of SS; higher-income retirees owe tax on up to 85% of SS. State treatment varies — most states exempt SS from state income tax.

SS Wage Base

The maximum annual wages subject to Social Security tax. For 2025: $176,100. Income above this is exempt from the 6.2% SS portion of FICA (but still subject to 1.45% Medicare). The wage base typically increases each year in line with average wage growth.

SSTB — Specified Service Trade or Business

Under §199A, certain service businesses cannot claim the QBI deduction if income exceeds thresholds. Includes: law, accounting, consulting, financial services, health, performing arts, athletics, and any business where the principal asset is the reputation/skill of employees. Does NOT include engineering, architecture, or most product businesses.

Standard Deduction

A flat amount all taxpayers can subtract from AGI regardless of actual expenses. 2025: $15,750 (single), $31,500 (MFJ), $23,625 (HOH). Extra $1,600 per person age 65+ or blind. Since TCJA 2017 doubled it, ~90% of filers take the standard deduction instead of itemizing.

Take-Home Pay (Net Pay)

Your gross income minus all taxes: federal income tax, state income tax, city income tax, FICA/payroll taxes, and any other withholding. The calculator's "Take-Home" in the pie chart shows annual take-home after all taxes — not after living expenses, retirement contributions, or health insurance premiums.

Tax-Loss Harvesting

Selling investments at a loss to offset capital gains or ordinary income (up to $3,000/year). Losses carry forward indefinitely. The wash-sale rule disallows losses if you buy the same or substantially identical security within 30 days before or after the sale. Crypto currently not subject to wash-sale rules.

Taxable Income

The final dollar amount to which tax brackets are applied. Calculated as: AGI − Standard Deduction − QBI Deduction (if applicable). This is the number used to look up your tax in the bracket table. Always lower than AGI and much lower than gross income. Appears on Line 15 of Form 1040.

TCJA — Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (2017)

Major 2017 tax legislation that: nearly doubled the standard deduction, cut rates, capped SALT at $10k, introduced the 20% QBI deduction, expanded the CTC, and cut the corporate rate to 21%. Many individual provisions were set to expire ("sunset") after 2025. The OBBB Act effectively extended most core TCJA provisions.

W-2 — Wage and Tax Statement

Form sent by employers by Jan 31 showing wages paid (Box 1) and federal taxes withheld (Box 2). Box 1 already excludes pre-tax 401(k) contributions. Box 2 is what you enter as "withholding" in the calculator. Social Security wages (Box 3) and Medicare wages (Box 5) may differ from Box 1.

W-4 — Employee's Withholding Certificate

Form you submit to your employer to adjust paycheck withholding. Key steps: Step 1 (filing status), Step 2 (multiple jobs), Step 3 (dependents), Step 4(a) (extra income), Step 4(b) (extra deductions), Step 4(c) (extra flat withholding per paycheck). The W-4 Advisor in this calculator calculates exactly what to enter in Step 4(c).

Wash-Sale Rule

Disallows a capital loss deduction if you buy the same or "substantially identical" security within 30 days before or after selling it at a loss. The disallowed loss is added to the basis of the replacement shares. Currently does NOT apply to cryptocurrency — a notable tax planning opportunity for crypto investors.

Withholding (Federal)

Federal income tax deducted from your paycheck by your employer each pay period and remitted to the IRS on your behalf. It's a prepayment toward your annual tax bill. W-2 Box 2 shows total withholding for the year. If withholding + credits > total tax liability, you get a refund; if less, you owe.

📦 The Four Rate Boxes at the Top

Effective Rate: Your actual average tax burden. Total federal income tax ÷ gross income. Example: $80k income, $12,514 tax = 15.6%. Much lower than marginal rate.
Marginal Rate: The tax rate on your last dollar of income. The bracket you've topped out at. A raise is taxed at this rate. Does NOT apply to all income.
Federal Tax: Total federal tax before any credits. Includes income tax + capital gains tax + SE tax + additional Medicare tax + AMT if applicable.
State Tax: Estimated state income tax based on your state's 2025 brackets applied to your federal AGI minus the state's standard deduction.
City Tax: (Only shown if applicable.) Local city income tax on top of state tax. NYC, Philadelphia, Detroit, and others charge residents directly.
AMT box: (Only shown if triggered.) The extra Alternative Minimum Tax owed above your regular tax.

✅ OBBB Above-the-Line Deductions Box

Shown when you have overtime, tips, senior deduction, SE tax deduction, or retirement contributions. Every dollar here reduces your AGI before the standard deduction even applies.

Overtime Deduction (OBBB §80001): The capped portion of your OT pay. Cap is $12,500 single / $25,000 MFJ. If your OT exceeded the cap, only the cap appears.
Qualified Tips Deduction (OBBB §80001): Full qualified tip income from customarily-tipped jobs. No dollar cap — only limited by phase-out above $150k.
Senior Deduction 65+: $6,000 per person age 65+. May be reduced if income exceeded the phase-out threshold.
50% SE Tax Deduction §164(f): The employer-half of your SE tax is deductible above-the-line. Automatically calculated.
Retirement / HSA Contributions: Your 401(k), IRA, and HSA contributions all reduce AGI dollar-for-dollar.
Total AGI Reduction: The sum — your AGI is lower by exactly this amount, saving you taxes at your marginal rate on every dollar.

💰 Retirement & Savings Tax Impact Box

Shows the tax savings from your pre-tax retirement and HSA contributions. The calculation is: contributions × marginal tax rate.

401(k) / 403(b) Pre-Tax: Each dollar contributed saves you your marginal rate. A $10,000 contribution at 22% = $2,200 in federal tax savings alone.
Traditional IRA Deduction: Only if you qualify (no workplace plan above income limits). Reduces AGI.
HSA Contribution: Triple tax benefit — reduces AGI now, grows tax-free, and never taxed if used for medical expenses.
Estimated Tax Savings: The approximate federal taxes saved from all pre-tax contributions at your marginal rate. State savings are usually additional.

🧾 Self-Employment Tax Breakdown Box

Only shown when SE Mode is on. Walks through how your SE tax and deductions are calculated.

Net SE Income: Your Schedule C profit — gross business revenue minus all business expenses. This is what SE tax is based on.
SE Earnings (×92.35%): The IRS reduces net SE by 7.65% before calculating SE tax (accounts for employer-half). Effective SE rate on gross = 14.13%.
SE Tax (15.3%): 12.4% SS (up to wage base) + 2.9% Medicare on all SE earnings. This is separate from income tax.
50% SE Deduction: Half of SE tax is deductible above-the-line — like how employers deduct their payroll tax share. Reduces AGI, saving income tax.
QBI Deduction (20% of net SE): Further reduces taxable income (not AGI) by 20% of business income. Applied after standard deduction.

🏛️ Social Security Taxability Box

Only shown when you entered Social Security benefits. The IRS doesn't tax SS the same as wages — it uses a special provisional income formula.

SS Benefits Received: Total annual SS/SSDI benefits you entered.
Provisional Income: AGI + tax-exempt interest + 50% of SS benefits. This determines how much SS is taxable.
Taxable Portion of SS: The dollar amount added to your taxable income. Maximum 85% of SS can ever be taxable — never 100%.
SS Included Rate: The percentage of your SS that was included (0%, 50%, or 85% as determined by the IRS formula).
Non-Taxable SS: The portion that is completely free of federal income tax. At minimum, 15% of all SS benefits is always tax-free.

⚠️ AMT Box

Only shown when your AMT calculation exceeds regular tax. Rare for most wage earners, but common for ISO stock option holders and very high earners.

AMTI (Alternative Min. Taxable Income): Your regular taxable income with certain deductions added back — including the standard deduction, SALT, and ISO stock option gains.
AMT Exemption Applied: The AMT "standard deduction" — $88,100 single / $137,000 MFJ (2025). Phases out at higher incomes.
Tentative Minimum Tax (26%/28%): AMT calculated at 26% on AMTI up to $232,600, 28% above. This is compared to your regular tax.
Regular Income Tax: Your ordinary + CG tax calculated through normal brackets. AMT applies because this was less than the Tentative Minimum Tax.
Additional AMT Owed: The difference — Tentative Minimum Tax minus Regular Tax. This extra amount is added to your bill.

💳 Credits Applied Box

Credits reduce your tax bill dollar-for-dollar — shown here in the order they're applied. Non-refundable credits first (reduce to $0), then refundable credits (can go below $0).

Non-Refundable CTC: The portion of the $2,200/child Child Tax Credit that reduces your tax bill toward $0. Cannot go below $0.
Other Dependent Credit (ODC): $500 per non-child qualifying dependent. Non-refundable — only reduces tax to $0.
Refundable CTC / ACTC (+$): The cash refundable portion of CTC. Up to $1,700/child. The +$ sign means it's added to your refund, not deducted from taxes.
Earned Income Credit (EIC/EITC) (+$): Fully refundable credit for lower-to-moderate earners. The +$ means it increases your refund directly.

📅 Quarterly Estimated Payments Box

🔑 Lesser-Known Tax Terms

Basis (Cost Basis): What you originally paid for an asset. Capital gain = sale price − basis. You must track basis for every investment you sell.
Step-Up in Basis: When you inherit an asset, your basis is reset to the fair market value at the date of the decedent's death — eliminating capital gains tax on lifetime appreciation.
Constructive Receipt: Income is taxable when you could have received it, not just when you actually did. A check in your mailbox on Dec 31 is that year's income even if uncashed.
Kiddie Tax: Unearned income (dividends, CG) above $2,500 for children under 19 (or full-time students under 24) is taxed at the parent's rate, not the child's lower rate.
Hobby Loss Rules: If a business is deemed a "hobby" (not profit-motivated), losses cannot offset other income. The IRS presumes profit motive if you made money in 3 of 5 consecutive years.
Passive Activity Rules: Losses from passive activities (businesses you don't materially participate in) can only offset passive income — not wages or investment income. Rental real estate is usually passive.
At-Risk Rules: You can only deduct losses up to the amount you personally have "at risk" in an investment (cash invested + recourse debt). Protects against paper losses from leveraged investments.
Depreciation: The systematic deduction of a business asset's cost over its useful life. IRS assigns depreciation schedules — commercial real estate: 39 years; residential rental: 27.5 years; equipment: 3–7 years.

🏢 Business Structure Tax Impacts

Sole Proprietor: All income is self-employment income. Pay SE tax (15.3%) + income tax. File Schedule C. Simplest but highest SE tax burden.
Single-Member LLC (SMLLC): Taxed identically to sole proprietor by default — disregarded entity. Provides legal liability protection but no SE tax benefit.
Multi-Member LLC: Taxed as a partnership. Each partner gets a K-1 showing their share of income, losses, and deductions. File Form 1065.
S-Corporation: SE tax only on your W-2 "reasonable salary" portion. Remaining profit distributed via K-1 — NOT subject to SE tax. Can save $5,000–$20,000+ per year for profitable businesses.
C-Corporation: 21% flat corporate rate on profits. Dividends paid to shareholders face double taxation (corporate tax + personal dividend tax). Rarely beneficial for small businesses.
K-1 (Schedule K-1): Tax form showing your share of income, deductions, and credits from a partnership, S-corp, estate, or trust. Flows to your personal return.
Net Operating Loss (NOL): When business deductions exceed income, creating a loss. Can be carried forward indefinitely to offset future income (80% limit per year post-TCJA).

🏠 Real Estate Tax Rules

Primary Residence Exclusion: Sell your home? Exclude up to $250,000 of gain ($500,000 MFJ). Must have owned and lived there 2 of last 5 years. Can use once every 2 years.
Rental Property Rules: Rental income is ordinary income. Deduct mortgage interest, property tax, insurance, repairs, depreciation (27.5 years). Net rental losses limited by passive activity rules.
Depreciation Recapture: When you sell rental property, the IRS recaptures depreciation deductions you took at a 25% rate — even if you sell at a loss relative to original cost.
1031 Exchange: Swap one investment property for another of equal or greater value and defer capital gains tax indefinitely. Must use a qualified intermediary and identify replacement property within 45 days.
Vacation Home Rules: If rented out 14 days or less per year — rental income is tax-free. If rented more, complex rules apply splitting expenses between rental and personal use.
Real Estate Professional Status: If you spend more than 750 hours/year in real estate activities and it's your primary occupation, rental losses are not passive — they fully offset ordinary income.

🌍 International & Special Tax Rules

Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (FEIE): U.S. citizens living abroad can exclude up to $126,500 (2024) of foreign earned income from U.S. taxes if they pass the Physical Presence or Bona Fide Residence test.
Foreign Tax Credit: If you paid taxes to a foreign country, you can take a dollar-for-dollar credit against U.S. tax on that same income. Prevents double taxation.
FBAR (FinCEN 114): If you had foreign financial accounts totaling over $10,000 at any point during the year, you must file an FBAR. Separate from your tax return. Penalties are severe for non-compliance.
GILTI: Global Intangible Low-Taxed Income — applies to U.S. shareholders of controlled foreign corporations. Ensures minimum U.S. tax on foreign profits.
Tax Treaty: Agreements between the U.S. and other countries that modify how certain income is taxed for residents of those countries. Can reduce withholding on dividends, royalties, and other payments.

🎁 Estate, Gift & Trust Taxes

Annual Gift Tax Exclusion: Give up to $18,000 per recipient per year (2024) tax-free and without using your lifetime exemption. Married couples can "gift-split" up to $36,000/recipient.
Lifetime Gift/Estate Tax Exemption: $13.61 million per person (2024). Gifts above annual exclusion reduce this lifetime amount. Estates below this are not federally taxable. This threshold is set to drop roughly in half in 2026 without Congressional action.
Estate Tax: Federal tax on transfer of wealth at death. 40% rate on estate value above the exemption. Only about 2,000–3,000 estates per year are subject to federal estate tax.
Irrevocable Trust: Assets transferred irrevocably outside your estate. Income taxed to trust or beneficiaries. Useful for estate planning and asset protection.
529 Plan Superfunding: Contribute 5 years' worth of annual gift exclusion at once ($90,000 single / $180,000 MFJ) to a 529 college savings plan without gift tax consequences.

⚕️ Health & ACA Tax Terms

Premium Tax Credit (PTC): Refundable credit for individuals who buy health insurance through the ACA Marketplace and have income between 100%–400% of Federal Poverty Level. Reconciled at tax time with any advance credit received.
Advance Premium Tax Credit (APTC): The portion of PTC paid in advance directly to your insurance company to lower your monthly premiums. Must be reconciled — if income was higher than estimated, you may owe some back.
HDHP (High Deductible Health Plan): Required to contribute to an HSA. 2025: minimum deductible $1,650 (self-only) / $3,300 (family). Out-of-pocket max: $8,300 / $16,600.
Flexible Spending Account (FSA): Employer plan allowing pre-tax contributions for medical expenses ($3,200 limit 2024) or dependent care ($5,000). Use-it-or-lose-it rules apply (with limited rollover). Unlike HSA, FSA doesn't require HDHP.
Dependent Care FSA: Up to $5,000 pre-tax for childcare costs. Reduces your AGI. Coordinates with (but can't double-count against) the Child & Dependent Care Credit.

❓ Common Taxpayer Misconceptions

MYTH: "A raise pushed me into a higher bracket — I take home less." FALSE. Only the dollars above the threshold are taxed at the higher rate. A raise always increases take-home pay.
MYTH: "A big refund means I'm good with taxes." FALSE. A large refund means you over-withheld — giving the IRS an interest-free loan. Aim for $0 balance.
MYTH: "SE tax and income tax are the same thing." FALSE. They are two completely separate taxes. Self-employed pay both on their net income.
MYTH: "Deductions and credits are equally valuable." FALSE. Credits reduce tax dollar-for-dollar. Deductions only save you your tax rate × deduction amount. A $1,000 credit = $1,000 tax reduction. A $1,000 deduction at 22% = only $220 tax reduction.
MYTH: "I can't contribute to both a 401(k) and an IRA." FALSE. You can contribute to both — up to $23,500 in a 401(k) AND $7,000 in an IRA in the same year. IRA deductibility may phase out if you also have a workplace plan.
MYTH: "Capital gains are always taxed at 20%." FALSE. Long-term CG can be 0%, 15%, or 20% depending on your total income. Many people qualify for the 0% rate on long-term gains.
